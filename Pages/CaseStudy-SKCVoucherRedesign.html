SK Chase Voucher Redesign

Timescale

The project spanned 5 months, but about 1 work day or less per week, with some pauses between development sprints.

Team involved

Me (Teo) - design, iteration and presentation of designs, communicating ideas to product manager and developer

Jan Harding - Product manager at SK Chase - outlining requirements, offering knowledge & insight into the system. Facilitating feedback on designs from team and client hotels. Liaising with client hotels.

Tom Van Enkevort - Developer at HappyPorch - implementation of designs. Deep knowledge of CMS and technical limitations, offering technical insight into designs and ensuring feasibility of implementation.

Brief

  • I was asked to re-design the printable Voucher and Evoucher for SK Chase to modernise them. The new designs should be aesthetically pleasing - suitable for customers to print at home and give as a gift.
  • The print version and E-voucher have different requirements and functionality, so they need separate design processes.
  • It must contain all the relevant information as in the current version.
  • The vouchers are printed on A4 paper, and each design needs to be foldable into at least 2, if not three to easily fit into an envelope.
  • A stretch goal, I was asked to design something which offers more possibilities for client customisation.

Problems/Constraints

  • One of SKChase’s clients requested a large image format to feature an enticing photograph of the offer purchased. I was asked to prioritise this request. Resultingly, there was significantly less usable space for the voucher information. A full reimagining of the way this info is presented was therefore needed.
  • Some information is optional to display and clients have the possibility to hide certain fields depending on their specific needs.
  • This meant the design had to accommodate variable informational fields and resize accordingly to avoid blank white space, or to accommodate the maximum possible number of informational fields.

Process

Research

I was provided with some samples of different vouchers that presented information in various ways. I researched ticket/voucher designs online. I looked at some competitors’ voucher designs, too.

Concept Development

Folding templates

SK Chase caters to many different hotels with diverse branding requirements. The information and branding displayed is pulled from various places within the CMS and automatically populates the print/e-voucher templates, so the design concept needs to accommodate variables such as the word count of personal message and voucher details.

To cater to a variety of preferences, I opted to create three different templates - one with an image taking up 50% of total space, one with 33% image and a low-ink version with no image.

I mapped out overlays of ½ and ⅓ folds while designing the layout of the page to ensure the folds don’t overlap content fields.

This can only be done approximately due to the variable lengths of the text fields and their ability to expand, but the folds still lie in the white space between fields.

I will develop a bar for the bottom of the page with modular fields containing all info, for maximum space efficiency. This will be used in all 3 formats for visual consistency and ease of use.

Solution

The general anatomy of the template can be split into three sections:

  • Decorative image (except for ink-saving version) and hotel logo
  • Voucher content & personal message
  • Booking information

Booking information component

My first priority was developing the field containing the booking information. I blocked out the necessary number of fields for the maximum amount of info, grouping fields by similarity. I ensured all the needed info was placed next to each other for ease of use.

The old design evenly distributed all of the booking information across the page, which is possible as it doesn’t feature an image. My idea was to make a design with a large image possible by condensing and organising all the booking information into a much smaller space, and utilising that space more efficiently.

I used PenPot flex boards to create the layout. This allowed me to mock up the concept using CSS flex. I used this to simulate how information fields re-format when different fields were hidden (opted out of by hotel clients), to mimic the behaviour of the final template. My aim was to account for all possibilities of hidden/visible fields so that the information bar would look aesthetically pleasing without any obvious blank spaces or overflow.

This set of illustrations was created for HappyPorch’s 2023 impact report. I was essentially left to my own devices with the brief for these, so I chose ‘busy hands’ as the guiding concept - inspired by this illustration from the previous year’s image. My two main visual elements for this set was hands and negative space. I also got the go ahead to add some additional skin tones, as these illustrations are transparent and displayed on a white background and I wanted to better reflect the diversity within our ‘busy hands’.
I wanted to push myself a bit with the negative space, and had to iterate a lot to come to the versions here. Balancing the light and dark elements and ensuring they didn’t look too busy or complicated took some time, and the micro whitespace within the illustrations took a lot of refinement.

Takeaways

  • This set of graphics have a relatively consistent (heavier) visual weight through the incorporation of the dark block shapes. They really feel like a ‘set’, and I think they complement each other well.
  • Although they’re not perfect, these demonstrate clear development of my technical skills as I take on increasingly demanding design ideas. I strive to continue pushing myself and the working with - not against - the limitations of this style in the future.
